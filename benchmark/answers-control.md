# Answers — CONTROL (no skill)

Same base model, no solana-brain loaded. Answered from general knowledge.

## Q1
A governance token for US + India users is a high-risk launch that needs work across legal, security, and engineering before next month. Concretely: (1) Legal/securities — a US governance token risks being deemed a security; get securities counsel on token design, avoid investment-contract framing (no profit promises, real governance utility), and consider geofencing or excluding US persons. India treats crypto as VDAs with a 30% gains tax plus 1% TDS on transfers and ambiguous legal status — get Indian counsel and tax advice. (2) Entity/tax — likely a foundation or offshore structure for token issuance; coordinate corporate, tax, and treasury. (3) Distribution — define allocations, vesting, lockups (use a vesting program, not ad-hoc transfers). (4) Mint — Token-2022 vs SPL, decide on mint/freeze authority (ideally revoke or move to multisig/DAO), metadata via Metaplex. (5) Security — audit the mint, vesting, and any claim/airdrop contracts; the claim flow is the classic exploit surface. (6) Governance mechanics — Realms/SPL Governance is the standard. A month is tight; sequence legal+tax first, then engineering+audit, then launch. Do not skip the audit gate.

## Q2
This is an active incident — treat it as one. First, stop the bleeding: pause or disable the claim endpoint immediately (kill the deploy, revoke the program's upgrade if needed, or disable the front end so no new victims interact). If the program has an admin pause/guardian, trigger it. Do not wait to understand root cause before halting. Second, contain: if a hot wallet or program authority is compromised, move remaining funds to a fresh secure wallet/multisig and rotate any exposed keys. Third, diagnose from on-chain data — pull the draining transactions, identify the exploited instruction (common culprits: missing signer/owner checks, unchecked account substitution, a claim that transfers from a vault PDA without verifying the claimant, replayable signatures, or an approval/delegate left open). Fourth, communicate: warn users to stop interacting and revoke approvals/delegates; be transparent. Fifth, after containment, write a root-cause analysis, get the fix audited before redeploying, and consider contacting an incident-response firm and, if large, law enforcement and exchanges to flag the attacker address. Preserve logs and tx hashes for forensics. Resist the urge to "quick-patch and redeploy" without review — that's how second exploits happen.

## Q3
At peak traffic, transactions fail to land mostly because of Solana's local fee market and leader-mempool dynamics, not a global queue. Common causes: (1) Priority fees too low — during congestion, validators order by compute-unit price, so transactions without competitive priority fees get dropped. (2) Blockhash expiry — a `recentBlockhash` is valid ~150 slots (~60–90s); if you sign early and submit late under load, it expires. (3) Bad compute budget — not setting `ComputeBudgetProgram` limits, so you either overpay headroom or get deprioritized. (4) RPC issues — public/shared RPCs drop or rate-limit transactions under load. Fixes: set priority fees dynamically using `getRecentPrioritizationFees` (or Helius/Triton fee APIs) targeting the relevant writable accounts; set a tight, accurate `setComputeUnitLimit` plus `setComputeUnitPrice`; fetch a fresh blockhash right before sending and use `lastValidBlockHeight` to track expiry. Use a reliable RPC with staked connections or a transaction-sending service (e.g., Helius sender, Jito bundles for ordering guarantees). Implement send-and-confirm with retries on the same signed transaction (re-broadcast, don't re-sign) until expiry, then rebuild. Monitor landed rate and tune fees against observed congestion.

## Q4
For a two-person team shipping a first program to mainnet next week, treat readiness as gates, not a wishlist. Security first: (1) Every instruction validates account ownership, signers, and that PDAs match expected seeds — no unchecked accounts. (2) Arithmetic uses checked math (no silent overflow); no unbounded loops; rent and account-init handled. (3) Authority model is explicit — who can upgrade, who can pause, ideally a multisig (Squads) on the upgrade authority, not a single hot key. (4) Get at least an external review or audit; for a real launch with funds, an audit is non-negotiable even on a budget — consider a smaller firm or a security review skill plus bug bounty. Testing: comprehensive unit + integration tests (LiteSVM/Mollusk/Bankrun), plus tests against a forked mainnet state (Surfpool) and a full devnet/testnet dry run with real wallets. Ops: monitoring and alerting on program errors and balances, an incident runbook, and a rollback/pause plan. Process: deploy with a verifiable build, document the program ID, lock down upgrade authority, and consider a staged rollout with caps on TVL initially. Finally, confirm key management — no private keys in code or env committed anywhere. Don't launch if the audit/security gate isn't met.

## Q5
For a multi-depositor vault, the core decision is how to track per-user balances. Two patterns: (1) Per-user PDA — derive a `UserDeposit` PDA from seeds like `[b"deposit", vault.key(), user.key()]`. This scales (no account-size limits), parallelizes well (different users touch different accounts, avoiding write-lock contention), and is the standard. (2) Single shared account with a map — simpler but hits account size limits and serializes all writes; avoid for many users. Architecture: a `Vault` config PDA (authority, total deposits, token mint), a vault token account owned by a PDA authority (so only the program can move funds via CPI with `invoke_signed`), and per-user deposit PDAs. Pitfalls: (a) Always verify PDA seeds match the passed accounts — never trust a passed account as "the user's." (b) Share accounting — if issuing vault shares, compute shares as `deposit * totalShares / totalAssets` and guard the first-deposit/empty-vault case to prevent share-inflation/donation attacks (seed with a dead-shares minimum). (c) Rounding always in the vault's favor, checked math throughout. (d) Rent — fund and handle init/close of user PDAs; reclaim rent on full withdrawal. (e) Reentrancy/CPI ordering — update state before transferring out. (f) Don't let an attacker substitute a different vault's token account.

## Q6
This is a legal question requiring securities counsel — what follows is informational, not legal advice. Whether your token is a security in the US turns on the Howey test: an investment of money in a common enterprise with an expectation of profit derived from others' efforts. Tokens marketed as investments, sold to fund a team's development, with profit expectations, lean toward being securities; tokens that are genuinely functional/consumptive or sufficiently decentralized (no central party whose efforts drive value) have a better non-security argument. The 2026 landscape is more crypto-favorable than prior years, but enforcement risk remains real — do not self-determine this. Engage securities counsel early and design the token to support the conclusion you want (real utility, no profit promises, decentralized governance, careful marketing). On incorporation: token projects commonly separate the operating company (often a US C-corp, e.g., Delaware, for fundraising and IP) from a token-issuing entity, frequently an offshore foundation (Cayman, Switzerland, Panama, BVI) chosen for governance neutrality and tax treatment. The right structure depends on your token model, investors, jurisdictions, and tax exposure — coordinate corporate counsel, securities counsel, and a crypto-experienced tax advisor (CPA) before issuing anything. Flag both counsel and CPA review as required gates.
